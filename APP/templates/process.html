Natural Language Processing

Natural Language Processing on Essays and Need Statements

NLP techniques including text classification, word frequency, and sentiment analysis were used to analyze the predictive values of "essays" and "need statements" (submitted by the teachers) on funding results. All the analysis were performed with 10% (113,828) of randomly selected data from the original database.

​

Conclusions

Both Naïve Bayes and Linear SVC Text Classification test results showed low F1 scores with high “false positives”. Naïve Bayes weighted average F1 score was 0.57 while Linear SVC was 0.62. Based on these test results, it is determined that ‘essays’ are not a good predictor of whether project requests will be funded.

Word Frequency Analysis - Keywords from need statements for funded and not funded projects were compared to explore their impact on funding results. Of the 500 most used words in both funded and not funded projects’ need statements, an overwhelming 448 or 90% words were shared by both lists. From the venn diagram below, you can see that there aren't significant differences in word frequency between funded and not funded projects. Therefore, need statements are also not a good predictor of whether project requests will be funded.

Sentiment analysis were performed on essays for all projects. Both funded and not funded projects averaged a highly positive sentiment compound score of 0.95, which indicates that most of the essays have a positive tone, and sentiment compound score is again not a good predictor of whether project requests will be funded.

In conclusion, all NLP analysis results are conclusive that data from written statements have no predictive value of funding success.

K Nearest Neighbors

K Nearest Neighbors Analysis

After testing several different types of models, our group decided to use a K Nearest Neighbors (KNN) model. Subsequent testing revealed that school poverty level, student grade level, students reached, and amount of money requested were the most predictive factors available for use in our data set. We preformed repetitive experiments before settling on a model where K Neighbors = 99.

Our model returns an approximately 70.8% accuracy score. Unfortunately, when analyzed using a confusion matrix, our data does return a fairly high rate of false positives. We suspect that this is the model’s attempt to compensate for features with low predictive value.

If we had additional time to pursue this project, we would delve deeper into secondary sources that might help us better explain trends in the data. We briefly explored the possibility that median income of the areas surrounding the school might help us better predict project success. When added to our existing pool of variables, however, it didn’t significantly improve our models.
